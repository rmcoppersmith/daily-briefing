Engineered Bacteria Turn Plastic Waste Into Parkinson's Medication

Scientists at the University of Edinburgh have engineered E. coli bacteria to convert plastic waste into L-DOPA, a key drug used to treat Parkinson's disease, potentially transforming a major pollution problem into a pharmaceutical supply chain.

The process breaks plastic down into terephthalic acid, which is then metabolized by the engineered bacteria to produce L-DOPA. Researchers say the same approach could be adapted to manufacture fragrances, food flavorings, and industrial dyes from plastic feedstock. If scalable, this biotechnology could simultaneously address plastic pollution and reduce pharmaceutical manufacturing costs, creating a circular economy pathway from waste to medicine.

Hubble Catches a Comet Exploding in Real Time

In an extraordinary stroke of luck, NASA's Hubble Space Telescope captured a comet shattering into four or five pieces in November. Scientists say the odds of witnessing a comet breaking up live through Hubble are "vanishingly small."

The footage could help solve a long-standing mystery: why long-period comets are more susceptible to catastrophic fragmentation than their short-period counterparts. The observation provides rare data on the mechanics of cometary disintegration, offering insights into the composition and structural integrity of these ancient solar system objects. Researchers plan to analyze the debris trail to determine what triggered the breakup.

Harvard Scientists Build a Chip That Can Twist and Control Light

Scientists from Harvard have built a tiny chip that can actively twist, tune, and control how light spins on the fly by stacking two photonic crystal layers, distinguishing between left- and right-circularly polarized light with near-perfect precision.

The breakthrough has potentially massive implications ranging from identifying dangerous pharmaceutical compounds to powering faster data networks. The device's ability to dynamically manipulate circular polarization states of light on a compact chip could enable new applications in drug safety screening, optical communications, and quantum computing. The photonic crystal approach offers a scalable manufacturing pathway compared to existing bulky optical setups.

Growing Evidence That Botox Can Reduce Depression and Anxiety

A growing body of research suggests Botox can meaningfully reduce symptoms of depression and anxiety by paralyzing the facial muscles that express sadness, breaking the brain's feedback loop that reinforces negative emotions.

Studies show reduced anxiety and higher happiness scores even in non-depressed patients receiving Botox treatments. The mechanism is rooted in the facial feedback hypothesis: by preventing the physical expression of sadness, the brain receives fewer signals to maintain the emotional state. While not yet approved as a mental health treatment, the accumulating clinical evidence is building a compelling case for investigating the world's most popular cosmetic procedure as a potential psychiatric intervention.
