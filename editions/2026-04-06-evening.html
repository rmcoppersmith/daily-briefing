Artemis II Is Flying Around the Moon Right Now — Crew Breaks 56-Year Distance Record

Today — Flight Day 6 of the Artemis II mission — four astronauts (Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, Jeremy Hansen) are executing the first crewed lunar flyby since Apollo 17 in 1972. At 1:57 PM ET today, the crew broke the record for the farthest distance any human has traveled from Earth, surpassing Apollo 13's mark from April 1970. Maximum distance of 252,760 miles occurs at 7:07 PM ET. The lunar flyby window runs 2:45–9:40 PM ET. Tech Brew flagged the mission in today's afternoon edition.

Orion entered the lunar sphere of influence at 12:37 AM EDT today, at the end of Flight Day 5. The ten-day mission launched from Kennedy Space Center on April 1, with the crew spending the transit period conducting suit demonstrations, deep-space systems operations, and preparing for the flyby. The spacecraft swings around the far side of the Moon before beginning the return journey to Earth, with splashdown expected in approximately 4 days. NASA is broadcasting the flyby live on NASA+. This is not a landing mission — Artemis III carries that mandate — but it marks the first time humans have been in the vicinity of the Moon in over 50 years, the farthest any person has traveled from Earth since the Apollo era, and a critical validation mission for Orion's systems ahead of lunar landing.
