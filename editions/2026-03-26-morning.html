AI Approach Uncovers Dozens of Hidden Planets in NASA's TESS Data

Astronomers at the University of Warwick have validated over 100 exoplanets — including 31 newly detected worlds — using a new AI pipeline called RAVEN applied to data from NASA's Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite. The tool analyzed observations of over 2.2 million stars collected during TESS's first four years of operations.

The researchers focused on planets orbiting close to their stars with periods under 16 days, providing the most accurate assessment of how common these short-period worlds are. Using RAVEN, they validated 118 new planets and identified over 2,000 high-quality planet candidates — nearly 1,000 of them entirely new. This complements NASA's ExoMiner++ system, a new version of the AI model trained on both Kepler and TESS data.

JWST Reveals Most Distant Red Galaxy Yet at Redshift 11.45

Using the James Webb Space Telescope, astronomers have discovered the most distant red galaxy ever detected — designated EGS-z11-R0 — at a redshift of approximately 11.45, meaning it existed just 400 million years after the Big Bang. Unlike typical high-redshift galaxies with blue UV slopes, this one shows significant dust attenuation and mature stellar populations.

The galaxy was serendipitously identified by analyzing JWST's Near Infrared Spectrograph data in the CEERS field. EGS-z11-R0 has a stellar mass of 1.6–4 billion solar masses and a star-formation rate of 10–40 solar masses per year. Its rising or approximately constant star-formation history suggests sustained growth rather than a short-lived burst, challenging models of early galaxy evolution and indicating dust-enriched environments existed surprisingly early in cosmic history.
