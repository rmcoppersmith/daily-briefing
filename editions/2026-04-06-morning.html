Netflix Open-Sources VOID: AI That Rewrites Physics When Removing Video Objects

Netflix Research released VOID, an open-source framework for removing objects from video while simulating the physical consequences of those edits — going well beyond existing tools that simply paint over backgrounds. The model distinguishes what to erase, what's physically affected, and what to preserve, then uses a judge model to chart downstream scene consequences.

VOID handles physics it was never trained on: demos show a balloon floating when its holder is removed and blocks remaining stable when one in a chain is erased. In head-to-head evaluations, 25 judges compared VOID against six baseline models including Runway, preferring Netflix's results nearly two-thirds of the time. This is Netflix Research's first public AI release — a sign that video production tools are moving beyond object erasure toward full simulation of real-world physics for precision production work.

For US Plastics Makers, Tariffs Are the Best of Times

While most industries struggle with tariff-driven cost increases, US domestic plastics manufacturers are in an unusual position of competitive advantage: protected from cheaper foreign plastic imports by the same Section 122 tariff framework that's hurting other sectors. Morning Brew flagged the dynamic as a rare tariff-era bright spot for American industrial producers.

The current 10% universal import surcharge under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 — effective since February 24, 2026 for 150 days — exempts USMCA-compliant goods but applies broadly to other foreign plastic products. US domestic producers effectively operate tariff-free relative to competitors importing finished plastic goods from outside the USMCA zone. The Iran war's upward pressure on crude oil (WTI at $111.96) does raise feedstock costs for domestic plastics producers, partially offsetting the competitive benefit — but the structural trade dynamic still favors US-based manufacturers.
