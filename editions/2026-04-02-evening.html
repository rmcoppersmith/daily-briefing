Investors Desert OpenAI for Anthropic on Secondary Markets

OpenAI shares are nearly impossible to sell on secondary markets, with roughly $600 million in shares sitting without a single buyer — while $2 billion in fresh capital floods into rival Anthropic instead. The stunning shift marks a potential inflection point in the AI investment landscape.

Bloomberg reported Wednesday that institutional investors holding OpenAI shares have been unable to find buyers across multiple secondary platforms. In contrast, buyers are reportedly sitting on $2 billion in cash ready to deploy into Anthropic. The defection runs deep: at least a dozen direct OpenAI investors, including Founders Fund, Iconiq, Insight Partners, and Sequoia Capital, have now also backed Anthropic. Business fundamentals are accelerating the shift — Anthropic's share of combined OpenAI-plus-Anthropic enterprise spend has surged from roughly 10% at the start of 2025 to over 65% by February 2026, according to Ramp Economics Lab. Anthropic also projects positive cash flow by 2027, a stark contrast to OpenAI's ongoing losses.

Public Launches First Agentic Brokerage — AI Agents Manage Your Money

Investment platform Public has unveiled the first agentic brokerage, allowing users to design AI agents that autonomously monitor markets, execute trades, and manage their portfolios. The launch video has gone viral with 2.3M views.

The process is simple: users define a task (e.g., "rebalance my portfolio monthly"), answer the AI's follow-up questions to tune the strategy, then monitor and modify their agents over time. The agents run on financial-grade infrastructure and Public maintains a full record of all transaction history for transparency and compliance. Early access is available by request. The launch represents one of the first consumer-facing deployments of fully autonomous financial agents.

Baidu Robotaxis Paralyzed Mid-Traffic by Cloud Failure in Wuhan

Over 100 Baidu Apollo Go robotaxis abruptly halted on Wuhan's busy expressways Wednesday after a cloud system failure — the second mass robotaxi shutdown in four months. Some collisions were reported, though no injuries. Chinese authorities attributed the incident to a "system malfunction."

The incident exposes what safety researchers have long warned: centralized cloud architectures create a single point of failure for entire fleets of autonomous vehicles. Last December, a San Francisco blackout knocked out traffic signals and caused Waymo's fleet to create a city-wide traffic jam. Neither incident resulted in serious injuries, but critics note there is no widely adopted contingency plan — in China or elsewhere — for what happens when a city's worth of driverless cars go dark simultaneously. Tech Brew's analysis frames this as the defining infrastructure challenge for the robotaxi industry in 2026.

Elon Musk's "Try Our Self-Driving" Post Reignites Safety Backlash

Elon Musk posted on X Wednesday: "Try out self-driving in a Tesla. It will greatly improve your quality of life and may save your life." The post immediately drew fierce criticism from safety advocates and engineers who say Tesla's Full Self-Driving is still a Level 2 system that requires constant driver monitoring.

The post comes as Tesla is actively fighting a California DMV ruling that found the automaker violated consumer protection law by misleading buyers with the terms "Autopilot" and "Full Self-Driving." Tesla's vehicles maintain Level 2 autonomy — handling steering and speed — but do not constitute true autonomous driving. According to Tesla crash tracking data, at least 65 fatalities have been linked to the Autopilot system since its 2015 launch. Critics on X were quick to point out the irony of the promotional post appearing the same week a California judge ruled Tesla broke the law with similar marketing claims. Tech Brew flagged the post as one of the top stories generating buzz in the tech community today.
