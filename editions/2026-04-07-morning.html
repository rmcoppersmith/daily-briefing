OpenAI publishes "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age" — a mini-New Deal for AI

OpenAI released a 13-page policy blueprint outlining how society should adapt to impending superintelligence — proposing robot taxes, a public wealth fund paying dividends to every American, a four-day workweek, expanded social safety nets, and containment playbooks for rogue autonomous AI. CEO Sam Altman told Axios the moment requires a new "social contract," saying the transition toward superintelligence has already begun.

The document, titled "Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age," compares this moment to the Industrial Revolution, arguing that the Progressive Era and New Deal had to rebuild the social contract for electricity and mass production — and AI may require something similarly ambitious. Key proposals include shifting the tax base away from labor and toward capital and automated labor taxes; seeding a sovereign-style wealth fund with AI company contributions that would pay dividends to all Americans (akin to Alaska's oil fund); piloting a 32-hour/four-day workweek; building an "AI trust stack" with stronger auditing, incident reporting, and public oversight; faster grid expansion; and containment protocols for autonomous AI systems that can't be shut off. OpenAI also announced fellowships, research grants of up to $100,000, up to $1M in API credits, and a new DC workshop to move ideas toward policy. Axios called it "the most detailed blueprint any tech titan has ever published for how to tax, regulate, and redistribute wealth from the technology he's building." Early reaction was a mix of "whoa" and "convenient." The Trump administration prefers a light-touch AI approach, but Senate bills to tax AI and mitigate job impacts are reportedly in the works. The proposal also addressed AI-enabled weapons threats — cyberattacks and engineered bioweapons — calling for expanded international safety cooperation.

Artemis II crew breaks Apollo 13 record — farthest humans have ever traveled from Earth

At 1:57pm ET Monday, the four Artemis II astronauts set a new record for the farthest humans have ever traveled from Earth, reaching 252,756 miles — surpassing the Apollo 13 crew's 1970 record by roughly 4,100 miles. The crew completed a lunar flyby including 40 minutes of planned communication blackout as they passed the moon's far side, and are slated to splash down on Friday.

Science X reported the final maximum distance as 252,760 miles. Canadian astronaut Jeremy Hansen said from the ship: "We most importantly choose this moment to challenge this generation and the next to make sure this record is not long-lived." The milestone puts Artemis II squarely in the history books and sets the stage for future crewed lunar missions. Morning Brew noted the crew did take photos during the journey — mostly for science rather than social media. The crew's safe return splash-down is scheduled for Friday.

Microscopic mechanism of 'quantum collapse' in real-world environments uncovered for the first time

Scientists have for the first time directly observed the microscopic mechanism behind quantum wavefunction collapse as it occurs in real-world (noisy, non-isolated) environments — a longstanding puzzle at the heart of quantum mechanics that underpins quantum computing and measurement theory.

Quantum collapse — the process by which a quantum system transitions from a superposition of states to a definite outcome upon measurement — has been theorized and described mathematically for a century, but its microscopic physical mechanism in realistic environments has remained elusive. This discovery has potential implications for quantum computing error correction and the fundamental interpretation of quantum theory.
