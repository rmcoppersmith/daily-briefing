Moonshot AI's Kimi: $18B Valuation, Zero Hierarchy, 300 Employees — No Titles or OKRs

Moonshot AI, maker of the Kimi K2.5 model recently found at the core of Cursor, has quadrupled its valuation to $18 billion. A rare inside look reveals an org running 300 people with no departments, no titles, and no OKRs — managed directly by co-founders, each overseeing ~50 people.

Moonshot's radical flatness is by design: they hire for "generalization ability" — the same metric they optimize their models for. Over half the staff have rotated through multiple roles. An agent fleet now completes in 90 minutes what previously took three people two days. But the model strains at scale — a former senior hire from big tech quit, saying the team was too young and too smart for their own good, and insiders worry the structure can't hold past 500 people. If it scales, Kimi's org chart becomes an industry blueprint. If not, it's an expensive experiment in radical management theory.

AI Makes Developers 19% Slower — But They Believe They're 20% Faster

A study found that developers using AI coding tools were actually 19% slower than without them — yet those same developers believed they were working 20% faster. The productivity paradox is one of the most striking findings in AI-assisted development research to date.

The perception gap — nearly 40 points between reality and belief — suggests that AI tools create a strong feeling of productivity even when actual output slows. Researchers attribute the slowdown to context switching, prompt iteration, and reviewing AI-generated code. The Code highlighted five practices that developers use to actually close the gap: tight scope definitions, atomic tasks, local testing before agent handoff, structured output formats, and explicit rejection of AI suggestions that don't meet standards on first review.
